Stephen Strasburg and The Birth of the Nationals¶

Introduction¶

Let me tell you a story, of a man who brought hope, joy, and glory to a city devoid of such those feelings for far too long. However, I must first set the stage before the show can begin. As America's national pastime, and my personal favorite sport, baseball has warmed the hearts and brought tears to the eyes for millions for over a century. Despite this, the unfortunate decision was made in 1971 to move the national pastime away from our nation's capital, when the Washington Senators relocated and becamethe Texas Rangers. For the next 34 years, D.C. remained dormant during the summer, fans waiting and praying that their beloved team would return. This day came in 2004 when the Montreal Expos financial failures pushed them to make a move to D.C., and the team known as the Washington Nationals were born.

Jacob Yank

What followed was disappointment and the usual woes of an expansion team. This storys protagonist is that pick, the 65 240lbs pitcher out of San Diego State, and the most hyped pitching prospect in baseball history, Stephen Strasburg. What comes next high, low, and everything in between. It is using the tools provided along the data science pipeline that will allow me tell this story clearly and correctly.

Part 2: Merry Strasmas!¶

After 59 wins in the 2008 season, the Nationals were the worst team in baseball, and were awarded the number one pick on the 2009 MLB Draft. As I spoiled it earlier, you know who they took, and immediately the hype machine began to build. Strasburgs 2010 debut with the Harrisburg Senators, the Nationals AA Minor League affiliate (the minor leagues are a developmental set of leagues where teams train their prospects before bringing them up to the majors), drew over 70 credentialed media members and ESPN nationally broadcast portions of the game.

My 9 year old self could not contain my excitement. Finally, my hometown team had some hope, and when news came that Strasburg would be making his major-league debut on June 8th of that year, I pleaded with my dad to take us. A burgeoning fan himself, he agreed, and on that day, at the nosiest of nose bleeds, we experienced the most electrifying performance of our lives.

Strasmas was born. Except, it didnt only come one a year, but rather every fifth day. Finally, after half a decade, there was hope. As seen above, the Nats went on to win 59 games again in 2009, and were awarded another number one overall draft pick. In the 2010 Draft they selected Bryce Harper, the greatest baseball prospect ever, period. He could have a whole project dedicated to him, but it was with Strasburg that this turn around really began.

We then limit the years to those in which Strasburg pitched. We use the groupby function to sum the stats of all pitchers throughout that time frame. In order to measure success, I'll use a simple but effective statistic. Strikeouts are a good measure of a pitchers success as they are independent of the 8 fielders around them. At its most simple level, it could thought of a win for the pitcher, as they, without outside influence, beat the batter they were facing. GS stands for games started, meaning where the listed pitcher was the first to pitch in the game. SO stand for strikeouts.

The table below shows 10 other pitchers who had more strikeouts that Strasburg during the 2012-2019 timespan. 7 of them won a Cy Young award during that time span, the award for the best pitcher in their respective league. The other 3 have won either the World Series MVP or their League Championship Series MVP. That is all to say, Strasburg is in excellent company.

The chart shoes Strasburg among all other player that have started a game during that time span. As we can see, only one pitcher has started fewer games and struck out more batters.

Another simple and common measure of pitcher success is ERA, earned run average, where a lower number is better. It is calculated as so, (earned runs/innings pitching)*9.

I write a function that will return a list containing the playerIDs corresponding to the remaining players in big_salaries.

This is done by using a regular expression to extract the player's first and last name from the big_salaries table. The next step is to pull the playerID from the people table using our regular expression matches. Finally, we add the playerID column.

In [24]:
import re
def get_player_ID(player_names):
    player_ID = []
    for player in player_names:
        name = re.findall('\w+', player)
        player_ID.append(people[(people['nameFirst'] == name[0]) & (people['nameLast'] == name[1])]['playerID'].iloc[0])
    return player_ID
big_salaries['playerID'] = get_player_ID(big_salaries.index)

What's Next?¶
